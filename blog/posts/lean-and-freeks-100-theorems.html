Lean and Freek’s 100 Theorems

2022-01-18T13:37:00-08:00

From the leanprover community website:

Freek Wiedijk maintains a list tracking progress of theorem provers in formalizing 100 classic theorems in mathematics as a way of comparing prominent theorem provers.

As you can see from the link (at least as of writing), currently Lean is in sixth place, with 63 theorems. Inspired by the idea that Lean could take over and become the one prover to (mostly) rule them all, I’ve been working on a theorem from the list which I think has a particularly nice proof, namely Bertrand’s postulate. My hope is that if Lean attains a new high score on the list, the field will pay attention and coalesce around it to some degree, and this will help progress move faster.

Is This Even a Good Idea?

https://imgs.xkcd.com/comics/standards.png

You might say that by supporting a new prover like this, I’m moving in the wrong direction. There will just be yet another prover to pick from and there will be less concentrated work on any one prover and progress as a whole will go down. Perhaps, but I take the optimistic view that Lean’s monorepo approach is a decisive factor that will make it easier to work with.

The remaining theorems

Here I list all the remaining theorems, lightly grouped by similarity, and roughly in order of how difficult I think they’ll be.

Stirling’s Formula

This is next for me after finishing Bertrand. I might like to do Robbins’ version of the explicit bound, since this seems useful.

Minkowski’s Fundamental Theorem

You Probably could complete this one in a few days, given a good understanding of mathlib. The hardest part is the measure theory part of “there are two points that overlap when modding out by \((2 \mathbb{Z})^n\)”. But even that should be a matter of cutting up the region, moving the pieces on top of the same square, and showing that if they don’t overlap, the resulting set has measure more than the square it’s contained in.

Induction

A few of these can be proved easily by induction on the construction of some mathematical object (or just on the naturals). These include:

  • Konigsberg Bridges Problem
  • Descartes Rule of Signs
  • Pick’s Theorem
  • The Ballot Problem

High school plane geometry Theorems

These include:

  • Pascal’s Hexagon Theorem
  • Feuerbach’s Theorem
  • Desargues’s Theorem
  • Theorem of Ceva
  • Morley’s Theorem

These all have to do with properties of various geometric constructions including conics, lines and polygons. It strikes me that many of them could probably be solved by reducing them to an algebraic statement about the coordinates of the points and using some advanced algebraic tactic such as nsatz.

Divergence of the Prime Reciprocal Series

The wikipedia proof for this honestly doesn’t look too hard.

Transcendental numbers

The Hermite-Lindemann Transcendence Theorem and “Pi is Transcendental” are highly related. The former implies the latter, which seems like a bit of an oversight. Whoever proves this, save yourself work by proving the general theorem first.

Solving Polynomials

“The Solution of a Cubic” and “The Solution of the General Quartic Equation” are both on the list. It seems pretty straightforward to just transcribe the solutions then simplify to prove they are zeros.

Normal Distributions

“The Central Limit Theorem” and “The Laws of Large Numbers”. They both are some notion of limits of distributions, so they seem related.

Euler’s Formula

Polyhedron Formula seems like a good target once there’s enough graph theory. The Number of Platonic Solids is a good followup.

Brouwer Fixed Point Theorem

Seems tricky, most of the wikipedia proofs involve homology, complex analysis or some other advanced topic.

Virgin Territory

Fermat’s Last Theorem, The Isoperimetric Theorem and the Fair Games Theorem have apparently never been formalized in any prover. FLT is obviously the gold standard (and to those of us formalists who have read Godel, Escher, Bach, it holds a special place in our heart). There is an effort in Lean to formalize a special case, so I’d say we’re on our way. The Isoperimetric Theorem strikes me as not too hard given the right multivariable calculus.
