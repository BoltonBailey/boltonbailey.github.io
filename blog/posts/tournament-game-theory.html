Tournament Game Theory

2020-08-08T00:37:19-07:00

This has no value I am aware of, but I think it could be a fun exercise to develop a mathematical theory around games like rock-paper-scissors and rock-paper-scissors lizard spock, and other generalizations of that idea.

Background and Problem Statement

A tournament is a directed graph where any two distinct vertices have exactly one directed edge joining them. Given a tournament, consider the symmetric, two-player, zero-sum, normal form game where the action sets are the sets of vertices of the tournament, and where, if the players moves are distinct, then a player receives +1 payoff iff the edge joining the moves originates on their chosen vertex. Call such a game a tournament game. We will refer to such a game and its related tournament interchangeably.

Conjecture A tournament game has a unique mixed strategy nash equilibrium.

Conjecture If a tournament game has a Mixed Strategy Nash Equilibrium with each action receiving nonzero probability, that MSNE is unique.

Proof attempts The set of MSNE is a convex polytope in the probability simplex, the intersection of \(\{x \in \Delta : x M_i \ge 0 \}\) for each action \(i\). Suppose this polytope is nontrivial.

  • If a game of the aforementioned form has a unique MSNE, this MSNE may have the property that all actions have nonzero probability of being played. This property is desirable since if there is a strategy with 0 probability, there is no reason to have a hand symbol for it in a format like rock-paper-scissors. Call such games non-simplifiable tournament games, or NSTG.
  • If no strategy dominates another, does this imply non-simplifiability? Prove that no strategy dominating another implies a cycle.

Classifying NSTGs

Let us enumerate a few NSTGs:

  • There is the trivial NSTG of order 1.
  • Rock-Paper-Scissors is the smallest nontrivial NSTG, with size 3.
  • There are no NSTGs of size 4.
  • but there are two of size 5 up to isomorphism.
    • Rock-Paper-Scissors-Lizard-Spock
    • Another game which can be thought of as a game of RPS where an additional RPS is played to decide a tie where both players threw rock.

The above points suggest two ways of constructing NSTGs:

  1. For any odd n, we may construct an NSTG where the action space is \(\mathbb{Z}_n\) and the winner is determined by \((a-b) \mod n\) is less than \((n-1)/2\). It can be seen that the unique MSNE assigns equal probability to all actions.
  2. Given an “index” NSTG \(I\), the action space of which indexes into a set of additional NSTGs \(\{X_i\}_{i \in I}\), we define a new coproduct NSTG on the action space \(\coprod_I X_i\), that is, the disjoint union of the action spaces of the \(X_i\). Let the winner between \((i_1, x_1)\) and \((i_2, x_2)\) be the winner of \(i_1, i_2\) in \(I\) if they are different, otherwise the winner between \(x_1, x_2\) in \(X_i\). The MSNE probability of \(i, x\) is the product of the MSNE probabilities of \(i\) and \(x\).

An interesting question is whether induction on the above two constructions yield all NSTGs up to isomorphism. It turns out to be false: Note that the above only gives us odd order NSTGs, but inspection shows that there is an NSTG of order 6.

This leads us to some more high-level questions:

  • Can we classify the simple NSTGs? (that is, the NSTGs that cannot be made from smaller NSTGs by the second construction above)
  • What is (a good way of computing) the number of isomorphism classes of NSTGs of a given order?
  • Can we use the fact that there must be a hamiltonian directed cycle?
  • Can we specify a normal form on the payoff matrix, then construct all valid matrices in linear time?
