Analysis of Choker, Part 1: The Betting

2020-05-19T21:45:47-05:00

This post analyzes the game Choker, which combines elements of chess and poker. The full rules are here. A visual explanation is given by this pdf (though this one doesn’t fully explain what happens if three queens and two rooks are dealt). This post explicitly deals with the betting stage of a choker hand. I also want to analyze the [placement and chess]({{<relref “./placement-and-chess.html”>}}) phase, but for now, this is a TODO.

Observations on the Possible Piece Sets

The order in which cards are dealt does not matter with respect to the piece set entering the placement phase. There are five types of pieces, any combination of which can be dealt (with the exception of five queens, since there are only 4 queens in the deck). By stars and bars, there are therefore $\binom{5 + 4}{5} - 1 = 125$ distinct hands. The Promotion, Demotion, Palace and Empress rules restrict the number of piece sets to 48 (or 47, see the “Becket Variation”).

Obviously important is the number of points in the final hand, since the hand with more points will usually win.

My programmatic analysis (code here) of the hands and their point values can be found in the tables section. Some summary statistics:

  • The mean point value is about 17.2, and the median is 17.
  • The smallest point value is 9, from a minor piece and four pawns (by demotion).
  • The largest point value is 29, from A Queen-Rook Empress or a Palace.

A few observations:

  • The Pawn-Pawn opener is actually an above-average hand in terms of points, due to the likelihood of one or multiple promotions in the subsequent draws. In fact, it’s even better than Rook-Pawn, presumably because at least one promotion is so likely and a second promotion queen is worth a lot.
  • The worst opener on average is Pawn-Minor (minor pieces are interchangeable in terms of score).
  • The best openers, as one might expect, are Queen-Rook and Queen-Minor, since Queen-Queen is a demotion.
